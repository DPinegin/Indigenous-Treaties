Treaty One


Background Information

Treaty One was established between the Imperial Crown of Great Britain and Ireland and the First Nations of the area.

Treaty one was negotiated and entered in August 1871, in Lower Fort Garry. The Treaty making process was set down during the Royal Proclamation of 1763.


Indigenous Communities that signed the Treaty

The First Nation Communities that signed Treaty One are: Peguis First Nation, Sagkeeng First Nation, Brokenhead Ojibway Nation, Roseau River Anishinaabe First Nation, Long Plain First Nation, Sandy Bay Ojibway First Nation and Swan Lake First Nation.








Historical Significance

Treaty One was the first signed treaty created in Canada. It is also the first numbered treaty. What makes Treaty One different from other Treaties is the fact that both Treaty One and Treaty Two had fewer clauses than the treaties that would follow.


The Historic Terms of the Treaty

The treaty laid out the boundaries of where the reserves would be for both settlers and Indigenous Peoples. Promises were made for schools too be built in each community, and an annuity of $3-$5 for each person registered under the Indian Act. In addition, items such as twine, ploughs, boards and oxen for some elected Chiefs, headmen and councilors.


Implementation of the Treaty in Current Time

Treaty One establishes clearly defined land ownership and jurisdiction, and foster a new relationship based on mutual respect, trust and understanding. In addition, they provide guidelines on how Europeans can use the traditional land that Indigenous Peoples have occupied. They also provide documents with Indigenous rights as written in the treaties.
